hello again,
we’re back.

it's me, emre kaan. i designed this site not as a tool, but as a simple and natural experience. every box, every gap, every shadow — all of them are in harmony with each other. i built both the code and the design myself. laying the foundation didn’t take long, but what truly took time was achieving balance. because to me, a design lives in the details you don’t immediately see.
the spacing between boxes, the softness of the corners, the size of the text, the transitions between colors, the glass-like transparencies, the harmony of shadows with light… all of it was part of a journey where i often spent time just searching for the right color code — sometimes for a single pixel. i tried to use the golden ratio wherever possible, especially in spacing and radii, so that everything speaks the same visual language. because i believe things that are pleasing to the eye are never a coincidence.
i don’t really like minimalism, but i do like simplicity. minimalism can sometimes feel unnatural. but simplicity is something closer to nature — it offers a kind of instinctive peace. that’s why i stayed away from harsh lines and metallic coldness. instead, i focused on softness, transparency, and a sense of discovery.

i didn’t try to describe myself while creating this site — instead, i let the design speak for me. because every detail, every choice reflects how i think and how i see the world.
code has always been something i could write. the real challenge is creating something that’s worth remembering. because great work isn’t just built with technology — it’s built with people who can think through it. maybe what we’ve built here is only significant to us, but its foundation rests on timeless principles that matter deeply.
charles babbage started a revolution, but it was ada lovelace who gave it a soul. she didn’t see machines as limited to numbers — she imagined a future where they could express music, text, and images. that vision lies at the heart of all the technology we hold in our hands today. i try to carry that vision forward. i believe design should not only be seen — it should be felt. that’s why i wrote this long note. otherwise, the tool and the technique could be introduced in a much shorter way. but what i want to share here is something different.

how it began

late university ~

i developed my own website, and while at first i thought i was just adding things i already had inside me, i realized throughout the process that i was actually rebuilding my ideas. i will keep improving my site because as artificial intelligence is trained on all available data, the number of new sites it can learn from is steadily decreasing, leading to a slow and clunky process. we need to do something to prevent the dead internet theory from becoming reality.

late university

with technovision and icontech, i found my true passion in computer science. i always believed code can be written anytime. our main goal is to create meaningful work; code is just a tool for that. there’s something beyond code. to create something amazing, code is necessary and a respected tool, but it’s not the beginning of the journey.

late high school

i wrote my first c programs and did some small tasks with hardware. more than the hardware itself, i was fascinated by the ideas it carried. i loved the notion that software, given enough time and knowledge, could solve even the most complex problems. that’s why i have always embraced the belief that we can create better systems.

early high school

i immersed myself in geographic explorations, which inspired in me a desire to discover rather than control. during this time, the idea of finding the best through various systems and tools—and the ideal of staying open-minded instead of fixed—took shape.

my path

technology isn't just about code; it's a tool for bringing a vision to life. our journey begins at an intersection where innovation and aesthetics merge, where technology transforms into art.
through projects like icontech, my goal isn't merely to offer solutions or pure information, but also to realize that seemingly unchangeable structures are, in fact, dynamic.

we can always be faster, stronger, and more technical, and these aspects certainly deserve respect, but they can always be achieved by someone. for me, the real challenge lies in thinking differently about what's right in front of us. we won't create something from nothing, but we will look at existing things with new eyes—that's where our creativity truly lies.
